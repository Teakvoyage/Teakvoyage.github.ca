Chinese Immigration

Gold Rush 1858

The "first major wave". The Chinese first appeared in large numbers in the Colony of Vancouver Island in 1858 as part of a huge migration from California during the Fraser Canyon Gold Rush in the newly declared Colony of British Columbia. Although the first wave arrived in May from California, news of the gold rush eventually attracted many people from China.

Head Tax 1885

From 1885, Chinese migrants had to pay a $50 “entry” or “head” tax before being admitted into Canada. The Chinese were the only ethnic group to pay a tax to enter Canada

Immigration for the railway, 1871-1882

Over 17,000 Chinese immigrants arrived in Canada to build the Canadian Pacific Railway. Many Chinese immigrants came to Canada for work and financial gain.

Exclusion Act, 1923

The Chinese Immigration Act, 1923, better known as the "Chinese Exclusion Act", replaced prohibitive fees with an outright ban on Chinese immigration to Canada with the exceptions of merchants, diplomats, students, and "special circumstances" cases.

Allowed Immigration 1947

With the Chinese Immigration Act, 1923 being repealed in 1947, the majority of immigrants in Canada emigrated from China, including Hong Kong, and Taiwan. From 1947 to the early 1970s, Chinese immigrants to Canada came mostly from Hong Kong, Taiwan, or Southeast Asia.

First Wave 1877-1928

The first wave of Japanese immigrants, called Issei (first generation), arrived between 1877 and 1928. By the 1930s, approximately 23,000 people of Japanese origin were living in Canada.

World War 2 (Japan's Entry) 1941

Shortly after Japan's entry into the Second World War on December 7, 1941, people of Japanese origin were forcibly removed from Canada's West Coast. This was a period of reduced immigration due to the discrimination.

Second Wave 1967

The second wave of Japanese immigration began in 1967, when immigration laws were amended and a point system was instituted. The 2016 census recorded 45,060 first generation Japanese Canadians.

First Immigration ~1900

Immigration from South Asia to Canada began in the late 19th century when a number of Sikhs from Punjab, India came to British Columbia to work in the lumber, mining, and railway industries, and later in agriculture. They were thought to be stealing jobs.

Continuous journey 1908

In 1908, the Immigration Act was amended to include the Government of Canada's "continuous journey regulation" which prohibited the landing of any immigrant who did not come to Canada by continuous journey from the country of which they were natives or citizens. This created a significant barrier to immigrants from South Asia as trips from most of the region included stops.

Komagata Maru 1914

On April 4, 1914, the Komagata Maru sailed from Hong Kong through Shanghai and the Japanese ports of Moji and Yokohama, finally arriving in Vancouver, British Columbia in May 1914. Its passengers, mostly Sikhs from Punjab, India, and all British subjects, challenged the continuous journey regulation of Canada's Immigration Act, which had been put in place in part to limit immigration from non-European countries. They were not allowed in.

Immigration reform ~1950-1960

Although the continuous journey regulation remained in effect until 1947, racial and national restrictions were removed from Canadian immigration regulations throughout the 1950s and 1960s. During this period, the size and diversity of South Asian communities in Canada expanded as immigrants from India and Pakistan sought opportunities in Canada's increasingly urban industrial society.

Vietnam War ~1975

Following the end of the Vietnam War, unrest in the region led many to emigrate from their countries of origin. By 1975, the people who were fleeing a number of Southeast Asian countries were often being referred to as "boat people" because Vietnamese people were using boats to flee their country.

Vietname War End 1975-1990s

Between 1975 and the early 1990s, Canada accepted some 200,000 of these refugees thanks to its immigration policies and the support of Canadians. Canadians across the country helped refugees resettle and rebuild their lives through sponsorship programs.
